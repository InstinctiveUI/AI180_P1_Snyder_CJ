# Written Reflection
## CJ Snyder | Game Design | AI 180

The bias I kept running into most was the Einstellung Effect — reaching for what I already know before considering anything else. I saw it first in math class, where I kept applying a familiar formula even when it stopped working. Then I saw it again in my AI session, where the first thing I did was tell the tool not to change my coding style. I was so locked into my own approach that I constrained the AI before it had a chance to show me something better. That is not a preference, it is a reflex.

What surprised me was how quickly that one bias triggered the rest. Because I had already committed to my style, I was primed to accept any fix that kept it intact. When the AI returned two different solutions, I accepted both without asking which was actually correct. That is confirmation bias at its most straightforward — I wanted the problem solved, the AI said it was solved, and I moved on. I skipped the step where I verify anything against an independent standard.

The anchoring bias sat underneath all of it. I opened the AI tool without deciding what I wanted it to do. With no goal defined ahead of time, the AI's first output became the benchmark for everything that followed. I was measuring my work against the AI's own suggestions rather than against something I had defined for myself. Kahneman's research on anchoring shows that the first number or idea introduced in any decision process has an outsized effect on judgment — I experienced exactly that (Kahneman, 2011).

The pattern across both phases is the same: I lead with familiarity, accept confirmation too quickly, and fail to define my goal before I start. Knowing that is the baseline. The next step is building a practice that interrupts it before it runs.

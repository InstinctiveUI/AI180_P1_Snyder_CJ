# Bias Comparison Annotation
## CJ Snyder | Game Design | AI 180

The same two biases show up in both phases: Einstellung and anchoring. In my studio work, Einstellung meant applying a known formula past the point where it was useful. In my AI session, it meant telling the tool not to deviate from my style before I had even seen what it could offer. The mechanism is identical — past experience blocking present evaluation — just applied to a different context.

Anchoring also crosses both phases. In my bias artifact process, I kept pulling new ideas back toward my original concept instead of letting them develop. In my AI session, I opened the tool without a goal, which let the AI's first output become the reference point for everything that followed.

Where they differ is in visibility. In studio work, I can usually feel myself getting stuck — there is friction, and I notice it eventually. With AI, the bias is invisible in the moment because the tool keeps moving. The AI returns confident output, I accept it, and the session looks productive even when I have not actually evaluated anything. That is what AI makes harder to see: it removes the friction that would otherwise signal that something is wrong.
